Summary

Pallister-Killian syndrome is a rare chromosomal disorder caused by the presence of at least four copies of the short arm of chromosome 12 instead
of the normal two. Major symptoms may include a coarse face with a high forehead, sparse hair on the scalp, an abnormally wide space between the eyes, a fold of the skin over the inner corner of the eyes, and a broad nasal bridge with a highly arched palate. Intellectual disability, loss of muscle tone, and streaks of skin lacking color are often present.

Symptoms
  • Extremely weak muscle tone
  • Distinctive facial features
    • Large forehead (a)
    • Depressed nasal bridge
    • Widely-spaced eyes
    • Low-set ears
    • Cupid's bow lip
    • Highly arched
    • Thin hair on the head and sparse eyebrows (b)
  • Intellectual abilities
  • Developmental and speech delays
  • Skin pigmentation differences (f)
  • Skeletal differences
    • Extra fingers and toes
    • Hip dislocation
    • Joints that are stiff or will not move
  • Heart defects

Testing and Diagnosis

  • Chromosome study of the blood
  • Chromosome study of the skin cells (fibroblasts)
  • Fluorescent in situ hybridization (FISH)
  • Additional imaging tests
    • X-rays
    • MRIs
    • CT scans
    • Ultrasound
  • Can be diagnosed before birth by amniocentesis
    or chorionic villus sampling (CVS)

Treatment

  • Currently there is no cure
  • Dependsing on the symptoms, treatment options can include:
    • Surgery to address medical conditions such as congential
      diaphragmatic hernia, heart anomalies, genitourinary conditions
      and cleft palate
    • Respiratory and breathing support
    • Physical, occupational and recreational therapy
    • Enrollment in early intervention programs to
      address development delays
    • Assisted communication devices and sign
      language training (top image)
    • Bracing, casting or surgery to address bone issues
    • Appropriate management of seizures if present

Long-term Outcome

  • Children with PKS will require coordinated, life-long medical care
  • Individualized instruction
  • In adulthood, many individuals will still remain dependent
    on parents and family members (bottom image)
  • Individuals with milder forms of PKS can live a more independent life
    at home or in a group-home setting
    • Ongoing support, encourgement and monitoring will be needed

Why Care?

Pallister-Killian Syndrome is a disease that occurs randomly and there is nothing you can do to prevent it. There are
no known environmental, dietary, or lifestyle factors that could cause it. There are kids being born into this world that
have never had a chance to live a normal life and along with this brings unwarranted stress to the family and friends of the child. A person should know about this disease and care about it since it can happen to anyone. Research is being done
to better understand the disease in molecular, cellular, and clinical studies.
